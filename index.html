a three.js particle system demo

A while back Radiohead open sourced their House of Cards music video data in the form of CSV files. The CSV files contain x, y and z coordinates for the points in each frame of the video. These points were captured with a special combination of lasers and sensors — more technically, the video data was collected with LiDAR technology. In this case, their LiDAR “camera” scanned 360° and had 64 individual lasers. Sensors retrieved the data and the scene was reconstructed in software. The production team also incorporated glass and mirrors to give the music video a distorted look (note the errant particles):

The video data currently lives in a GitHub repository. The nature of the data has lent itself well to reconstructing the video with programs like Processing. There may also be some VR applications. With the video data freely available, I was able to take the very first frame of the video (thousands and thousands of points) and render it as a particle system in the browser using three.js, a 3D JavaScript library. As someone who took a computer graphics course in college, I can appreciate how much simpler three.js made working with WebGL. I gave the demo trackball controls such that the particle system could be explored from all angles and distances.

I've always been interested in the methods behind this music video. As far as I know, it's the only music video to use realtime 3D recording. The vibe of the video fits in perfectly with the theme(s) from In Rainbows and I'd say the director put it well:

“In a weird way it's a direct reflection of kind've where we are in society — everything is data … It seems like our lives are digital and so in that sense it definitely felt apt.”

This demo can be rotated, panned, and zoomed with the usual touch gestures, or by dragging and scrolling with a mouse if you're on a desktop.
